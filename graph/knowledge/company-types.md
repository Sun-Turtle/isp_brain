# Company Types for UK ISPs

## Company Limited by Guarantee (CLG)

No shares, no shareholders. Members guarantee a nominal amount (typically £1) on winding up. Profits reinvested.

**Used by**: Green ISP (founded 2003, Hebden Bridge, ~2 staff, £34.50-£70/mo for FTTP, running on Openreach BT Wholesale + LLU via Opal/Tiscali. Ethical Consumer Best Buy 4 times. Solar-powered office, carbon positive.)

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Structure IS the mission — not-for-profit baked in | No equity investment (no shares to sell) |
| No shareholder pressure to extract or grow fast | Harder to borrow (no share capital) |
| Profits reinvested = long-term sustainability | Bank accounts can be trickier to open |
| Green ISP proves 22-year viability for an ISP | Founder capital via director's loan, not shares |
| Same £12 incorporation, same Ofcom registration | |

**Best for**: Mission-driven ISPs. Proves you can run lean with ~2 people and compete on values.

---

## Private Limited Company (Ltd)

Standard UK company. Has shares, shareholders, directors. Can distribute profits as dividends.

**Used by**: Andrews & Arnold (A&A), Aquiss, and most UK ISPs.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Maximum flexibility | Legal duty to maximise shareholder value |
| Can raise investment via shares | Mission can drift — no structural protection |
| Tax-efficient dividends (salary + dividends split) | "We're ethical" is marketing, not baked in |
| Well understood by banks, suppliers, Ofcom | |
| Cheapest and fastest to set up | |

**Best for**: For-profit ISPs, founders who want an exit path.

---

## Community Interest Company (CIC)

A limited company locked to a community purpose. Asset lock, dividend cap, regulated by CIC Regulator.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Asset lock protects mission even if sold | CIC Regulator approval + ongoing reporting |
| Eligible for grants and social investment | Community interest statement needed |
| Can issue shares (CIC Ltd by shares) | Dividend cap limits investor appeal |
| Credibility with ethical consumers | Overhead for a 5-person ISP |

**Best for**: Community broadband projects (e.g. B4RN rural fibre). Over-engineered for a small reseller.

## Reality check: startup capital for a reseller ISP

A CLG doesn't need startup capital from outside investors because the costs are low:
- £12 Companies House fee
- £2-5k legal/compliance review (optional for lean start)
- Monthly OSS/BSS platform fees (not upfront)
- Router stock is optional (3PL drop-ship or customer-provided)

Founder puts money in as a director's loan. Revenue repays it. No equity given up.

## CLG flexibility after incorporation

All key details are adjustable:

- **Members**: can be added or removed at any time via the internal register of members. No mid-year Companies House filing — just reflect changes on the next annual confirmation statement.
- **Registered address**: file an AD01 form online (£0, 24-48 hours). Can change the day after incorporation.
- **Company name**: directors pass a resolution, members approve, file NM01 form (£8 standard, £15 same-day). Must check name availability first.

The only thing that's genuinely hard to undo is choosing the wrong company type — switching a CLG to a Ltd requires dissolving and re-forming.
